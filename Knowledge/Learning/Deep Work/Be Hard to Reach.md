# Be Hard to Reach

> **Purpose**
>
> This document explains Cal Newport's strategy of becoming **hard to reach**. Rather than allowing email and electronic communication to dictate attention, this approach redesigns communication habits to protect deep work and reduce unnecessary shallow work.

---

# Source

## Primary Source

- *Deep Work* — Cal Newport

---

# Executive Summary

Modern knowledge workers often feel controlled by their inboxes.

The expectation that every message deserves an immediate response creates a constant stream of interruptions and shallow work.

Cal Newport argues that this situation is not inevitable.

By deliberately redesigning communication habits, individuals can regain control over their attention while still communicating effectively.

---

# Core Mental Models

## Communication Has a Cost

Every email creates work.

The cost is not only writing a reply but also the attention required to read, remember, and revisit the conversation.

---

## Protect Attention Like a Limited Resource

Attention should be invested intentionally.

Not every request deserves equal access to your time.

---

## Close Loops Early

Investing a little more effort in a reply today can eliminate many future interruptions.

---

# Source-Derived Knowledge

## The Big Picture

Author John Freeman observed that many people treat email as an unavoidable part of modern work, believing that resistance is futile.

Cal Newport rejects this assumption.

He argues that individuals have much greater control over electronic communication than they often realize.

Instead of surrendering attention to the inbox, communication habits can be redesigned to better protect deep work.

---

# Strategy 1 — Make Senders Do More Work

## Principle

The default expectation is that anyone can send an email and expect a response.

This creates a large volume of shallow requests.

---

## How It Works

Introduce a **sender filter** that requires the sender to invest additional effort before contacting you.

This encourages self-filtering and reduces unnecessary communication.

---

## Example from the Source

Antonio Centeno requires visitors to:

- read an FAQ
- confirm they searched for answers first
- acknowledge they are not sending spam

before a contact form becomes available.

---

## Purpose

The objective is to reduce unnecessary messages while setting clearer expectations about communication.

---

# Strategy 2 — Do More Work When Replying

## Principle

Quick replies often create long email chains.

Instead of responding only to the immediate question, consider the entire project the communication supports.

---

## Process-Centric Communication

Before replying, ask:

> What process will bring this conversation to completion as efficiently as possible?

---

## Example from the Source

Instead of replying:

> "When are you free?"

Provide:

- available dates
- available times
- next steps if none of those times work

The reply anticipates future questions and reduces unnecessary back-and-forth communication.

---

## Why It Matters

A slightly longer response today may eliminate multiple future emails and reduce unresolved mental commitments.

---

# Strategy 3 — Don't Respond to Every Email

## Principle

Not every message requires a reply.

The sender shares responsibility for making communication worthwhile.

---

## Practical Guideline

According to the source, consider not responding when:

- the message is ambiguous
- the topic is not relevant
- no significant positive or negative outcome depends on replying

---

## Trade-Off

Reducing unnecessary responses may occasionally cause:

- temporary confusion
- mild frustration

However, protecting attention allows more time for work that creates substantially greater value.

---

# PKP Curated Knowledge

## Why This Matters

Communication should support productive work rather than dominate it.

By reducing unnecessary communication and improving the quality of necessary communication, more uninterrupted time remains available for deep work.

# Domain Examples

The following examples illustrate how the concept can be applied within this PKP. These are contextual examples and are not claims made by the source.

---

## .NET Architecture

Instead of responding to many short architecture questions individually:

- document common decisions
- share reference documentation
- answer recurring questions once

---

## AI Engineering

Instead of discussing experiments through long message threads:

- prepare structured experiment notes
- communicate results with clear next steps

---

## Smart Home

Instead of handling repeated troubleshooting questions individually:

- maintain installation guides
- document automation procedures
- reference existing documentation

---

## Finance

Instead of repeatedly discussing investment decisions through messages:

- maintain an investment journal
- document decision criteria
- reference previous analyses

---

## Learning

Instead of answering the same learning questions repeatedly:

- create reusable PKP knowledge documents
- reference existing notes
- expand documentation when needed

---

# Practical Reflection Questions

Consider periodically:

- Which communications repeatedly interrupt deep work?
- Could some messages be prevented through better documentation?
- Am I creating unnecessary follow-up emails?
- Which conversations could be completed more efficiently?

- Protecting attention creates more opportunity for deep work.
