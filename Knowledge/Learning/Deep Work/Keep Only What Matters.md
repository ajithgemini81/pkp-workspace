# Packing Party

> **Purpose**
>
> This document captures Ryan Nicodemus's **Packing Party** concept and Cal Newport's adaptation of it in *Deep Work*. The technique is presented as a practical experiment for identifying unnecessary physical possessions and, more importantly in the context of *Deep Work*, unnecessary digital tools that consume time and attention.

---

# Source

## Primary Sources

- *Deep Work* — Cal Newport
- Ryan Nicodemus — Packing Party

---

# Executive Summary

People often retain possessions or digital tools because of the fear that they *might* become useful in the future.

The Packing Party replaces speculation with evidence.

Rather than deciding whether something is valuable, temporarily remove it from your life and observe whether you genuinely need it.

In *Deep Work*, this idea is adapted into a **30-day social media experiment** designed to evaluate whether network tools genuinely contribute to meaningful work and life.

---

# Core Mental Models

## Evidence Over Assumption

Do not ask:

> "Could this be useful someday?"

Instead ask:

> "Did I actually need it?"

---

## Temporary Removal Reduces Bias

Temporary absence provides objective evidence that is difficult to obtain through hypothetical reasoning.

---

## Fear of Missing Out (FOMO)

People frequently overestimate the importance of possessions and digital services because they fear losing potential future value.

The experiment replaces fear with direct experience.

---

# Source-Derived Knowledge

## The Original Packing Party

Ryan Nicodemus developed the Packing Party as a method for simplifying physical possessions.

Instead of deciding item by item what to discard, he packed nearly everything he owned into moving boxes.

During the following week:

- Items that were needed were unpacked and returned to normal use.
- Items that remained in boxes demonstrated that they were not part of everyday life.

The untouched items were then removed because actual experience showed they were unnecessary.

---

## Problem It Solves

People often keep possessions because of uncertainty.

Typical thinking:

> "What if I need this one day?"

This uncertainty creates unnecessary physical and digital clutter.

The Packing Party addresses this by requiring real evidence of usefulness rather than speculation.

---

## Digital Packing Party (Deep Work)

Cal Newport applies the same principle to network tools.

Instead of packing physical possessions, temporarily remove social media and similar digital services.

### Step 1

Disconnect completely from all social media services for 30 days.

---

### Step 2

Do not deactivate accounts.

Do not announce your departure.

Simply stop using the services.

---

### Step 3

After 30 days, evaluate each service by asking:

1. Would the previous thirty days have been notably better if I had been able to use this service?

2. Did people actually care that I was absent?

---

## Outcome

If both answers are **No**, the book recommends permanently leaving the service.

If the answers are clearly **Yes**, the service may be worth continuing to use.

The purpose of the experiment is to replace fear of missing out with direct evidence.

# PKP Curated Knowledge

## Why This Matters

The Packing Party is an example of using experimentation instead of assumption.

Rather than attempting to predict value, temporarily remove an activity and evaluate its actual contribution.

This approach can help identify distractions that compete with deep work.

---

# Domain Examples

The following examples illustrate how the experimental mindset can be applied within this PKP. These are organizational examples and are not presented as recommendations from the source.

---

## .NET Architecture

Possible experiment:

Temporarily stop monitoring low-value technical news feeds and instead spend the same time studying architecture books or building reference implementations.

Observe whether learning quality changes.

---

## AI Engineering

Possible experiment:

Pause frequent exploration of newly released AI tools and focus on mastering one established framework or model for a fixed period.

Evaluate whether depth of understanding improves.

---

## Smart Home

Possible experiment:

Reduce time spent making cosmetic dashboard adjustments and instead concentrate on improving automation reliability or reusable ESPHome components.

Compare the resulting value.

---

## Finance

Possible experiment:

Temporarily reduce consumption of daily financial news and dedicate that time to reviewing long-term investment principles or maintaining an investment journal.

Evaluate whether decision quality changes.

---

## Learning

Possible experiment:

Temporarily stop collecting new learning resources and focus on completing and synthesizing existing materials into permanent PKP knowledge documents.

Observe whether retention and understanding improve.

---

# Practical Reflection Questions

After completing a temporary removal experiment, consider:

- Did I genuinely miss this activity?
- Did its absence negatively affect my goals?
- Did removing it create additional time for higher-value work?
- Is this activity contributing to deep work or fragmenting attention?
